Drawn Together

Author: Minh Lê

Read about how a boy and his grandfather break the language, age, and culture barriers between them through art, play, and imagination.

“A beautifully told and illustrated story about a grandson and grandfather struggling to communicate across divides of language, age, and culture.”
–Viet Thanh Nguyen, Pulitzer Prize winner for The Sympathizer

2019 Asian/Pacific American Award for Literature

Hooks

Tell students you will show them a photo of a family. Display the photo and have students look for clues that help them understand who the family members are, what they are doing, and how they feel about each other.

Introduce the Big Question

How can I connect with people in my life?

Write the Big Question on chart paper and read it aloud. Explain that when you connect with someone, you have a strong relationship with that person. You feel like you belong together, and you care for and understand each other. Tell students that enjoying time together watching a movie, talking, and playing are all ways that people can connect with each other.

Have students name a person they enjoy spending time with, such as a family member or close friend. Ask: How do you feel when you are with that person? What do you do together? Use a word web like the one below to capture students’ thoughts and ideas. Display the word web throughout the lesson.

Author’s Craft

Visual Narrative

Explain that authors use illustrations in stories for many reasons. For example, to show what is happening, to express feelings and emotions, or to give the reader information that the words don’t say. Tell students that at the beginning and end of Drawn Together, the author only uses illustrations to tell the story. So, they will need to look closely at the illustrations to figure out what is happening, and to understand what the characters are thinking, wondering, and feeling.

Use the Activity Support to help students explore how pictures can tell a story.

Second Read Aloud

  • 1. Before reading, hold up Drawn Together and remind students that they have already read this book. Ask student volunteers to tell which part of the story was their favorite or most interesting. Then tell students that now they will reread the story and focus on how the boy and grandfather connect through their art.
  • 2. During reading, periodically stop and ask students questions about the characters and guide them to gain a deeper understanding of how the boy and grandfather connect through drawing.
  • The boy draws himself as a wizard. What does the grandfather draw himself as? (a warrior) How are their art styles different? (the boy’s is more colorful; the grandfather’s has straight lines and is in black and white.)
  • Stop at the page that says “… and we build a new world that even words can’t describe.” How do the boy and the grandfather use their imagination as they draw together? (the boy imagines he is wizard; the grandfather imagines he is warrior; they use their imagination to create a world for the wizard and the warrior)
  • What do the boy and his grandfather realize as they draw together? (they have more in common than they thought; they can play together and communicate through their art.)

Author’s Craft

Visual Narrative: Symbolism

Ask: What do you think of when you see a heart? Most people think of love when they see a heart. When an object, like a heart, is used to mean something else, it is called symbolism. Remind students that the author of Drawn Together uses illustrations to show what is happening in the story. He uses a dragon as a symbol to represent the differences between the boy and the grandfather. Guide students to understand how the author uses the dragon to symbolize those differences and the distance they create between the boy and his grandfather.

- We first see the dragon in the movie that the boy and his grandfather watch. How does the boy feel about the movie? (He doesn’t like it.) How do you know? (by the expression on his face) What do you think the boy is thinking? (that he and his grandfather will never understand each other)
- Where do we see the dragon next? (in the boy’s and grandfather’s drawings)
- Notice how the dragon begins to get bigger and bigger. What happens? (The dragon gets between the boy and grandfather.)
- How does the boy feel? (He worries that he and his grandfather might not understand each other again.)
- What do the boy and his grandfather do? (They draw a bridge; they turn the dragon into a bridge)
- What does the bridge do? (It brings the boy and grandfather together again.)
- How is the bridge a symbol? (It represents the boy and grandfather coming together again and growing closer.)

Third Read Aloud

1. Before Reading Hold up the book and ask students to read aloud the title with you. Remind them that the boy and grandfather form a connection through drawing together. Then explain that they will take a deeper look at the drawing tools the grandfather and boy used and what this tells us about their connection.

2. During Reading As you page through the book, ask students questions to elicit ideas about what the drawing tools reveal, such as:

  •  What does the boy use to draw? (markers; colored pencils; crayons) What does the grandfather use to draw?(paintbrush and black ink)
  •  In the beginning, what is the wizard holding? (a wand) What is the warrior holding? (a paintbrush)
  •  What happens when the warrior and wizard get separated? (the wand is on the warrior’s side and the paintbrush is on the wizard’s side; they use each other's tools to fight the dragon)
  •  As they use each other's tools to fight the dragon, how do you think they feel? (strong; not afraid anymore) Why? (they are working together)
  •  What do you notice about the drawing tools at the end of the story? (they are holding each other's drawing tools) Why is this important? (it shows that they have made a connection; they will hold on to this connection)

Mirrors & Windows

Explain that the title Drawn Together has more than one meaning in the story. Tell students that one meaning is that the boy and grandfather’s “new world” was drawn by both of them. Then explain that “drawn together” also means to bring people closer together. Display the page showing the boy and grandfather hugging goodbye. Discuss what drew the boy and grandfather closer together. For example, spending time together and their shared love of art.

After discussing the boy and his grandfather, tell students to think about the drawing they did before reading the story. How is their relationship with the person in their drawing similar to the boy and his grandfather? How is it different? Have students discuss with a partner using the prompts and sentence frame below. 

  • Who did you draw in your picture? What are you doing together?
  • How does this draw you closer?
  • How is this similar to and different from the characters in Drawn Together? 

This is my ________ and me. We like to ________ together. This draws us closer because ________ .

*If students need more prompting, you may provide some student exemplars. 

Student Exemplars:

“This is my best friend and me. We like to play soccer together. This draws us closer because we work as a team to score goals.”

“This is my dad and me. We like to make pancakes together. This draws us closer because we get to spend time cooking and eating breakfast together.”

 

Project-Based Learning

How can I connect with people in my life?

Have students choose one of the project ideas below. Allow students to choose other project ideas as long as the project addresses the Big Question. Then, distribute the Project Rubric to students.

Project-Based Learning Ideas

  • Be the Author: The beginning of the story does not have any words. Students pretend they are the author. They write or dictate sentences that describe what is happening and what the boy and grandfather are thinking and feeling.
  • Perform a Scene: Students “act out” their favorite scene without using any words. They use pantomime, gestures, and facial expressions to show what is happening and how the characters feel.
  • Wizard or Warrior? Students imagine what kind of character they and a friend might choose to be. They draw those characters and write or dictate a sentence about each one.

Share & Celebrate

Prepare to Share

Provide time for students to practice sharing their culminating project, reminding them that each partner should have a significant role in sharing the information. Partners should work collaboratively and follow agreed upon rules, norms, and protocols.

Use the bulleted points below to model good speaking and listening skills.

  • When I share my project, I look at the audience. I don’t keep my eyes down.
  • As I speak, I make sure that everyone can hear what I am saying.
  • I pronounce each word clearly and correctly.
  • I do not rush, but speak clearly and at a natural pace.
  • I pay attention with my eyes and ears when others are speaking.

Celebrate!

Have students share their projects with the class. As students share, be sure to model and encourage strong listening skills.

Plot Overview

A young boy arrives at his Thai grandfather’s house for a visit. The boy’s grandfather is very excited to see the boy. As they eat dinner and watch a movie, the two try to communicate, but because they do not speak the same language, they must find another way to understand each other.

After a while, the boy gives up trying to talk, gets his art supplies, and begins to draw. When the grandfather sees what the boy is doing, he gets an idea. The grandfather gets his own art supplies and begins to draw alongside the boy. The two begin to form a strong bond as they create a whole new world. In their newly created world, however, a dragon emerges that threatens to split them apart again.

The grandfather quickly swaps their drawing tools, and the two work together to transform the dragon into a bridge that brings them together again. At the end of the story, the boy, his grandfather, and mother are all overjoyed to find that the boy and his grandfather’s shared love of art has provided them with a way to connect without words.

Theme Overview

Connecting Across Differences Drawn Together explores the challenges and rewards of connecting with someone across different languages, generations, and cultures. A boy and his grandfather try without success to communicate and connect with each other as they eat and watch a movie, leading to awkwardness and unease. When they begin to draw together, however, they discover a deeper and more compelling connection that goes beyond words.

Activity Support

Unlock a Key Sentence

Display and read aloud the key sentence. Then guide students in unlocking its meaning by discussing each sentence chunk using the protocol described below.

Start with the sentence chunk that tells who or what the sentence is mostly about. Continue to unpack meaning and connect ideas, chunk by chunk, using the guiding questions in the table below.

And in a FLASH— | we see each other | for the first time.

Sentence Chunk Discuss Meaning
we see each other -Who is the sentence about? (we)
- Who is “we”? (the boy and his grandfather)
- “Each other” means that two or more people are behaving the same way toward one another. What are the boy and grandfather doing? (seeing each other)
-Does the word see mean that they are just looking at each other? (No, it means they are now understanding each other.)
for the first time - Is it really the first time the boy and his grandfather have seen each other? (No. They have been together all day but have not yet connected or understood each other)
- What does this phrase help the reader understand? (that until now, the boy and his grandfather have felt more like strangers)
And in a FLASH— - If something happens “in a flash,” how does it happen? (very quickly; instantly)
- What else flashes? Have you seen something that flashes?
- What happens so quickly in the story? (the boy and his grandfather suddenly understand each other and bond with each other)
- Is this part of the sentence needed? Why does the author include it? (to build excitement)
- Why does the author use all capital letters in the word FLASH? (to emphasize how quickly it happened)

After unlocking the key sentence, have students craft their own sentence using the following sentence frame:

And in a FLASH— __________ .

Activity Support: Visual Narrative

  • 1. Display a familiar story that students have read in class.
  • 2. Have students retell the story and explain how the characters feel about what happens.
  • 3. Tell students that now they will retell the story using only pictures. Assign each student one part of the story (beginning, middle, or end) and have them draw a picture to show it. Remind them to include what happens and how the characters feel.

Activity Support

Activity Support: I notice, I think, I wonder

1. Display an anchor chart with these sentence frames:

  • I notice _____ .
  • I notice _____ .
  • I notice _____ .

2. Point to each sentence frame and choral read aloud with students. Explain to students that they will use the sentence frames to discuss key events as they read the story.

3. Display the first two pages and point out that there are no words. Point to the illustrations as you describe important details.

4. Then, use a think-aloud to model how to use the sentence frames. For example, “I notice the grandfather is smiling. I think he is happy to see the boy. I wonder why the boy doesn’t smile.”

5. After modeling, ask students what they notice, think, and wonder. Encourage students to use the sentence frames when they respond.

6. Continue reading, using the sentence frames to discuss key events as follows:

  •  What do you notice, think, and wonder when the boy and grandfather are eating? (I notice that the boy is eating a hotdog and the grandfather is eating noodles; I think they are sad that they can’t talk to each other; I wonder what the grandfather is saying)
  •  What do you notice, think, and wonder when the boy and grandfather are watching a movie? (I notice that they are sitting apart from each other; I think the boy doesn’t like the movie; I wonder why he doesn’t like the movie)
  •  What do you notice, think, and wonder as the boy and grandfather draw together? (I notice that they both like to draw; I think they are happy that they found something they can do together; I wonder what else they will draw)
  •  What do you notice, think, and wonder when the boy is leaving the grandfather’s house? (I notice that they are hugging each other; I think that now they know each other better; I wonder how the boy and his grandfather will feel next time they see each other)

Note: Students' responses will vary. Accept all ideas and encourage students to explain their thinking.

 

Activity Support

Symbolism Activity

Remind students that the author uses a dragon in the story to symbolize the distance, or differences between the boy and his grandfather. Have students name other symbols they are familiar with, such as symbols for the words yes and no, peace, first aid, poison, money, freedom, etc.

  • 1. Create index cards with symbols that students are familiar with. You may also include familiar emojis.
  • 2. Give each student a card. If necessary, more than one child may have the same symbol.
  • 3. Describe a symbol. For example, say: “I’m thinking of a symbol that represents happiness.”
  • 4. The student that has the card with the symbol that represents happiness should hold up the card.
  • 5. Play until all students have held up a card. Mix up the cards and play again.

Activity Support

Author’s Craft: Word Sort

In this activity, students work together to complete a word sort and discuss the author’s use of adverbs to convey emotion and describe vivid details.

Follow the steps:

  • 1. Display the following list of adverbs from the text: fiercely, quickly, quietly, gradually, soundlessly, desperately, silently, slowly, wildly
  • 2. Using the chart below, have pairs or groups of students work together to sort the list of adverbs into three categories.

     

    Describes Sound Describes Speed Describes Intensity

    *Answer key is included below.

  • 3. Review student responses and discuss how adverbs might be used to describe vivid details and/or convey specific emotions (e.g. desperately conveys an intense emotion).
  • 4. Display the following sentences from the text and tell students to rewrite each sentence using a different adverb. Students may use adverbs from the word sort or other adverbs they know.
    • The wheels in Naima’s mind were spinning wildly. (p. 25)
    • Desperately Naima tried to turn the rickshaw, but she couldn’t seem to change direction. (p. 31)
    • Slowly, Mother eased a bangle over her hand and handed it to Father. (p. 46)

  • 5. Have students compare their sentences with a partner and discuss how different adverbs affect the meaning and emotion conveyed in each of the sentences.

    Answer Key:

    Describes Sound Describes Speed Describes Intensity
    quietly quickly fiercely
    silently gradually wildly
    soundlessly slowly desperately
